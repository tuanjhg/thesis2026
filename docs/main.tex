\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
\vspace{20pt}

\textbf{Problem.}
Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attacks against data-centre
networks can exhaust uplink bandwidth, transport-layer state, and
application-layer resources within seconds of onset
\cite{mirkovic2004taxonomy,sharafaldin2019cicddos}.  Although Network
Function Virtualization (NFV) and Service Function Chaining
\cite{etsi_nfv_architecture,sfc_rfc7665} provide the elasticity needed to
deploy mitigation functions on demand, closed-loop defence mechanisms in
production NFV environments remain predominantly reactive.  The
cumulative latency of telemetry collection, classification, policy
evaluation, Cloud-native Network Function (CNF) instantiation, and
traffic-steering enforcement results in an
\emph{attack-to-enforcement window} that exceeds the attack onset time
by one to two orders of magnitude \cite{rfc8612}.

\textbf{Approach.}
This thesis proposes \padonap{}, a four-stage closed-loop architecture
aligned with the functional decomposition of the Open Network
Automation Platform (ONAP) \cite{onap_docs}.  The framework couples a
dual-track machine-learning analytics engine with graduated
orchestration.  Track~A applies an XGBoost classifier
\cite{xgboost_chen2016} with Extra-Trees-based feature selection
\cite{geurts2006extratrees} to a five-class operational taxonomy derived
from CICDDoS2019 \cite{sharafaldin2019cicddos}; Track~B applies a Stacked
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network \cite{lstm_hochreiter1997} to
minute-level aggregated traffic variables in order to forecast attack
onset at one-, five-, and fifteen-minute horizons.  The probabilistic
outputs of both tracks are translated into five graduated response
tiers --- \emph{Normal}, \emph{Alert}, \emph{Pre-position},
\emph{Mitigation}, and \emph{Isolation} --- by a policy engine that
records every decision together with its SHAP feature attribution
\cite{shap_lundberg2017} as auditable metadata.  Mitigation actions are
gated by a Linear-Programming (LP) allocator
\cite{boyd2004convex} that enforces per-tenant Service-Level-Agreement
(SLA) bandwidth floors during active mitigation.

\textbf{Findings.}
On the CICDDoS2019 holdout partition, the Track~A detector attains a
balanced accuracy of 0.9504 and a macro-F1 score of 0.9361 with a
99th-percentile CPU inference latency of 2.2~ms; the Track~B forecaster
meets the configured AUC-ROC threshold of 0.80 at the one-minute
horizon.  End-to-end measurements on a Mininet fat-tree emulation
testbed \cite{alfares2008scalable,lantz2010mininet} and a real ONAP
Operations Manager (OOM) deployment show that proactive Tier-2
pre-positioning reduces first-action CNF instantiation latency from
6{,}006~ms (Tier-3 scrubber) to 505~ms (Tier-2 rate-limiter) in the
synthetic harness, and from 22{,}110~ms to 612~ms on the production
ONAP cluster.  The LP allocator preserves the configured
Ultra-Reliable Low-Latency Communication (URLLC) bandwidth floor in
every evaluated window.

\textbf{Implications.}
These results indicate that combining short-horizon traffic forecasting
with explainable, tiered, ONAP-inspired orchestration can substantially
reduce the attack-to-enforcement window without compromising
multi-tenant fairness or operational auditability.
Chapter~\ref{ch:eval} discusses limitations regarding the achieved
Track~A raw-accuracy gap, the single-replication design of the
scenario experiments, and the planned improvement roadmap.

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Motivation}
Modern data center networks (DCNs) host latency-sensitive and
mission-critical services that require continuous availability.
Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attacks remain a persistent threat
because they can exhaust link capacity, transport-layer state, and
application-layer resources through coordinated traffic sources and
heterogeneous attack vectors \cite{sharafaldin2019cicddos}.  In
multi-tenant DCN environments, this threat is amplified by shared
infrastructure: an attack targeting one service may consume shared
compute, network, and security-function capacity, thereby degrading
legitimate tenants that are not direct attack targets.

Network Function Virtualization (NFV) and Service Function Chaining (SFC)
provide mechanisms for deploying and steering traffic through software-based
security functions, such as firewalls, rate limiters, and traffic scrubbers
\cite{etsi_nfv_architecture,sfc_rfc7665}. Cloud-native deployment platforms
further simplify the packaging, scaling, and observability of these functions
\cite{kubernetes_docs}. However, elasticity alone does not eliminate the
attack-to-enforcement latency of a reactive defense loop. Such a loop must
observe suspicious traffic, classify or score the event, trigger a policy
decision, submit an orchestration request, wait until the mitigation function
is ready, and finally enforce traffic steering or filtering. During this
exposure window, attack traffic may continue to degrade service quality,
consume shared resources, and increase the probability of SLA violation.

This thesis is motivated by the operational gap between AI-based DDoS
analytics and orchestrated mitigation. Many learning-based approaches report
classification accuracy, F1-score, or forecasting error in isolation, but the
operational question is whether the warning signal provides sufficient lead
time for mitigation, whether the decision can be explained to policy logic,
and whether the resulting closed loop reduces service degradation under
realistic orchestration delays. This thesis addresses this gap by combining
near-real-time flow-level detection, multi-horizon attack forecasting,
explainable policy metadata, and an ONAP-inspired closed loop that maps
analytics outputs to graduated mitigation actions through DCAE-like
collection, policy decision, SO-like orchestration, and CNF-based enforcement
\cite{onap_docs}.


\section{Research Questions}
\label{sec:research-questions}

The design and evaluation of \padonap{} are guided by six research
questions that motivate the contributions enumerated in
Section~\ref{sec:contribution-highlights}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1 -- Proactive mitigation under orchestration delay.}
    Can a defence framework shorten the effective
    attack-to-enforcement latency in NFV/CNF environments by exploiting
    short-horizon attack-onset forecasting to prepare mitigation
    functions \emph{before} reactive evidence accumulates?  This
    question targets the response path that, in purely reactive
    designs, sequentially traverses telemetry collection,
    classification, policy evaluation, CNF instantiation, readiness
    checking and traffic steering.

    \item \textbf{RQ2 -- Forecast reliability under operational cost.}
    How can multi-horizon forecasting be combined with confidence-aware
    escalation rules so that proactive actions are triggered with a
    controlled false-positive rate, given that incorrect escalation
    consumes finite compute and network capacity and may itself degrade
    legitimate traffic?

    \item \textbf{RQ3 -- Explainable policy translation.}
    How can the probabilistic outputs of a learning-based detector and
    forecaster be translated into graduated, auditable orchestration
    actions, such that each escalation is accompanied by interpretable
    evidence \cite{shap_lundberg2017} that supports post-event
    forensics and operator trust?

    \item \textbf{RQ4 -- ONAP-compatible closed-loop integration.}
    How can the functional roles of an ONAP closed loop --- DCAE-like
    event ingestion, Policy-like decision making, Service-Orchestrator
    (SO)-like lifecycle control, and CNF-based enforcement
    \cite{onap_docs} --- be implemented in a research testbed while
    preserving deployability on a production ONAP cluster?  In
    particular, which components must be implemented natively, and
    which can be safely emulated without weakening the operational
    claim?

    \item \textbf{RQ5 -- Resource-aware mitigation in multi-tenant
    infrastructures.}
    How can the trade-off between attack suppression and
    legitimate-traffic preservation be formalised so that mitigation
    decisions do not introduce self-inflicted SLA violations on
    co-located tenants?  This question becomes critical in
    shared-resource data-centre networks where the CNF that protects
    one service consumes capacity required by others.

    \item \textbf{RQ6 -- Evidence-based evaluation.}
    How should the system be evaluated to separate the contribution of
    the analytics layer from the engineering overhead of the
    orchestration platform, yielding measurements that are reproducible
    from the released artefacts and that distinguish measured results
    from simulated approximations?
\end{itemize}

The mapping between these research questions and the contributions
introduced in the next section is as follows: RQ1 motivates
Contribution~1; RQ2 is addressed jointly by Contributions~1 and 3; RQ3
motivates Contribution~3; RQ4 motivates Contribution~2; RQ5 motivates
Contribution~4; and RQ6 motivates Contribution~5.

\section{Objective and Contents}

The primary objective of this thesis is to design, implement, and evaluate
\padonap{}, an AI-augmented NFV/CNF orchestration framework for proactive
DDoS mitigation in data center networks. The thesis investigates how
near-real-time detection, multi-horizon attack forecasting, explainable policy
decisions, and cloud-native service orchestration can be combined to reduce the
attack-to-enforcement latency of traditional reactive mitigation systems.

Specifically, \padonap{} aims to shift the defense paradigm from purely reactive
mitigation to proactive mitigation preparation. Instead of waiting until attack
traffic fully exceeds a mitigation threshold, the proposed framework uses both
immediate flow-level detection and short-horizon traffic forecasting to generate
early warning signals. These signals are then translated into graduated policy
actions, such as alerting, pre-positioning, mitigation, and isolation, through an
ONAP-inspired closed-loop architecture. The framework further considers
multi-tenant service requirements by incorporating resource-aware decision logic
for mitigation deployment and traffic handling.

To achieve this objective, the thesis covers the following contents:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A detailed analysis of DDoS mitigation challenges in data center
    networks, with emphasis on multi-tenant resource sharing, orchestration delay,
    CNF/VNF readiness time, and the limitations of purely reactive defense loops.

    \item The design of the \padonap{} architecture, including telemetry
    collection, AI-based detection and forecasting, explainable policy decision
    logic, ONAP-inspired closed-loop orchestration, and Kubernetes-based CNF
    enforcement.

    \item The development of a dual-track AI analytics engine that combines an
    immediate multi-class DDoS detection model with a multi-horizon forecasting
    model. The detection component identifies current attack states, while the
    forecasting component estimates near-future traffic risk to support proactive
    mitigation preparation.

    \item The design of a policy translation mechanism that maps AI outputs into
    auditable response tiers. This mechanism records relevant decision metadata,
    such as detection confidence, forecasted risk, selected mitigation tier, and
    policy rationale, to improve transparency and operational traceability.

    \item The implementation of a lightweight ONAP-inspired orchestration loop,
    including DCAE-like telemetry ingestion, Policy-like decision making,
    SO-like orchestration control, and CNF-based mitigation enforcement in a
    Kubernetes environment.

\end{itemize}

\section{Contribution Highlights}
\label{sec:contribution-highlights}

This thesis provides the following key contributions to the field of network
security and NFV/CNF orchestration:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Proactive DDoS mitigation through multi-horizon
    forecasting.}
    This thesis proposes a proactive mitigation framework that integrates
    near-real-time DDoS detection with a multi-horizon forecasting model to
    generate early warning signals before attack traffic reaches its most
    damaging stage.  The forecast outputs are used to support mitigation
    preparation actions, such as alerting, pre-positioning, and pre-scaling of
    lightweight mitigation functions.

    \item \textbf{ONAP-inspired closed-loop orchestration for CNF-based
    defense.}
    The thesis designs and implements a lightweight ONAP-inspired closed loop
    that connects telemetry collection, AI analytics, policy decision making,
    orchestration control, and Kubernetes-based CNF enforcement.  The design
    follows DCAE-like event ingestion, Policy-like decision logic, SO-like
    orchestration, and CNF-based mitigation while clearly distinguishing
    implemented components from simulated or proposed ONAP functions.  A
    selected real ONAP Operations Manager (OOM) integration test is included
    to quantify the deployment cost on a production-grade control plane.

    \item \textbf{Explainable translation from AI outputs to mitigation
    tiers.}
    The thesis introduces a policy translation mechanism that maps detection
    confidence, forecasted risk, and traffic indicators into graduated
    response tiers, including alerting, pre-positioning, mitigation, and
    isolation.  Every decision is accompanied by SHAP feature attributions
    and structured metadata, supporting auditability, operational
    traceability, and post-event analysis.

    \item \textbf{SLA-aware resource arbitration during mitigation.}
    \padonap{} formalises the mitigation-versus-SLA trade-off as a linear
    program that allocates link capacity between tenants and the mitigation
    CNF.  When the program is feasible, the allocator preserves every
    configured floor; when it is infeasible, the policy layer is notified so
    that the operator sees an explicit decision rather than a silent SLA
    breach.

    \item \textbf{Evidence-based evaluation across synthetic, emulation and
    real ONAP settings.}
    The framework is evaluated on three complementary settings: a
    deterministic synthetic harness, a Mininet fat-tree emulation testbed,
    and a selected real ONAP OOM integration scenario.  This separation
    isolates the contribution of the AI analytics from the operational
    overhead of a production orchestrator and yields measurements that can be
    reproduced from the released code base.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Thesis Outline}

The remainder of this thesis is structured as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch:background}} introduces the fundamental
    background concepts required for this work, including DDoS attack taxonomy,
    data center network characteristics, NFV and SFC mechanisms, ONAP
    closed-loop orchestration concepts, Kubernetes-based CNF deployment, and
    machine learning principles for detection and forecasting.

    \item \textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch:related}} reviews related work on ML-based
    DDoS detection, traffic forecasting, NFV/SFC-based mitigation, and
    closed-loop security orchestration. The chapter identifies the research gap
    between AI-based attack analytics and operational mitigation under

\section{DDoS Attacks in Data Center Networks}
\label{sec:ddos-bg}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Data Center Networks (DCNs) host latency-sensitive and mission-critical
services, including web applications, APIs, storage systems, enterprise
platforms, and internal network services. These services are expected to remain
available even when the network experiences sudden traffic surges or malicious
activity. A Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attack attempts to reduce
service availability by sending traffic from multiple sources toward a victim
service or infrastructure component. Such attacks may exhaust link bandwidth,
server processing capacity, connection state, queueing resources, or
application-layer resources.

In traditional isolated deployments, the impact of a DDoS attack is often
discussed mainly in terms of the direct victim. In DCNs, however, the effect
can spread beyond the attacked service because multiple applications, tenants,
or service domains may share the same physical and virtual infrastructure.
Compute nodes, switching capacity, ingress gateways, load balancers,
monitoring systems, and security functions may all be shared. Therefore, an
attack against one service domain can consume common resources and degrade
other legitimate services that are not direct attack targets. This
shared-resource coupling is one of the main reasons why DDoS mitigation in
DCNs must be studied not only as an attack classification problem, but also as
an orchestration and resource management problem.

\subsection{DDoS Attack Taxonomy}
\label{subsec:ddos-taxonomy}

DDoS attacks can be grouped into several major families according to the type
of resource they attempt to exhaust. A classical taxonomy distinguishes attacks
by target resource, traffic behaviour, degree of automation, source dynamics,
and defense mechanism requirements \cite{mirkovic2004taxonomy}. This taxonomy
is important because different attack families produce different traffic
signatures and may require different mitigation actions.

\textbf{Volumetric attacks} attempt to overwhelm the available bandwidth or
packet-processing capacity of the victim network. Examples include UDP floods,
ICMP floods, and reflection or amplification attacks. These attacks are often
characterized by high packet rates, high byte rates, abnormal protocol
distributions, and sudden changes in traffic volume.

\textbf{Protocol-exhaustion attacks} exploit finite protocol state in servers,
firewalls, load balancers, or connection-tracking systems. A common example is
a SYN flood, where many half-open TCP connections are created to exhaust the
server's connection table or intermediate network devices. These attacks may
not always require the same bandwidth as volumetric attacks, but they can
cause severe degradation by exhausting stateful resources.

\textbf{Application-layer attacks} target application logic or server-side
processing resources. HTTP floods and slow-rate attacks are examples of this
category. They are more difficult to detect because they may resemble
legitimate user behavior at the network layer. Instead of relying only on raw
packet volume, detection systems may need to observe request patterns, timing
behavior, flow duration, and statistical deviations from normal traffic.

In this thesis, DDoS taxonomy is not only used for describing attacks. It also
defines the operational label space of the AI pipeline and influences how the
policy layer selects mitigation actions. For example, a UDP flood may be
mitigated by rate-limiting or filtering based on transport features, while a
slow-rate attack may require application-aware inspection or stricter
connection handling.

\subsection{DDoS Impact on Availability and SLA}
\label{subsec:ddos-sla-impact}

The operational impact of DDoS attacks can be measured through several service
quality indicators. These include throughput degradation, increased latency,
packet loss, request failure rate, connection timeout rate, and reduced service
availability. In shared-resource DCN environments, the impact should also be
evaluated from the perspective of legitimate services or tenants that are not
direct attack targets. For example, if a mitigation function consumes excessive
CPU or bandwidth while protecting one service, other services may still
experience degraded performance.

This observation motivates the SLA-aware design of \padonap{}. A DDoS defense
system should not only detect attacks accurately, but also respond in a way
that preserves legitimate service quality where resources are available. This
requires coordination between AI analytics, policy decisions, orchestration
actions, and resource allocation.

\section{CICDDoS2019 and Window-Based Traffic Representation}
\label{sec:cic-bg}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Machine-learning models for DDoS detection require labelled traffic data that
captures both benign traffic and diverse attack behaviours. CICDDoS2019 is a
widely used benchmark dataset for evaluating DDoS detection and classification
approaches. It was introduced to address limitations of earlier intrusion
detection datasets by providing modern DDoS attack scenarios, a DDoS taxonomy,
flow-based traffic records, and feature-based evaluation support
\cite{sharafaldin2019cicddos,cicddos2019_official}. The dataset includes
multiple DDoS attack families, including reflection/amplification attacks,
UDP-based floods, SYN-based attacks, and WebDDoS scenarios, making it suitable
for evaluating multi-class DDoS classification in the context of this thesis.

Instead of training directly on raw packets, many DDoS detection systems use
flow-level or window-level statistical representations. This design is common
in network intrusion detection because aggregated features can summarize
traffic volume, protocol behaviour, address and port distributions, connection
patterns, and timing characteristics while reducing the cost of processing
individual packets. CICDDoS2019 follows this flow-based design: raw traffic is
captured as PCAP data and processed into CSV files using CICFlowMeter, which
extracts statistical flow features such as flow duration, packet counts, byte
counts, and packet-length statistics \cite{cicddos2019_official,cicflowmeter_official}.

Window-based representation is particularly relevant to \padonap{} because the
closed-loop orchestration layer does not act on every packet individually.
Instead, it consumes telemetry summaries and inference events over recent time
intervals. A window-level representation therefore provides a bridge between
traffic observation, AI inference, policy decision making, and orchestration
actions. This design is also consistent with recent DDoS detection and
mitigation surveys, which show that statistical traffic features and
machine-learning methods remain widely used in SDN and automated defense
systems \cite{su2024ddos_sdn_survey,apostu2025ai_ddos_survey}.

\subsection{Window-Level Feature Groups}
\label{subsec:window-features}

The AI pipeline implemented in this thesis uses a compact set of window-level
features derived from flow records. These features are not introduced as a new
public dataset or a new feature-extraction standard. Rather, they are selected
to represent feature families commonly used in flow-based DDoS detection:
traffic rate, entropy, protocol distribution, TCP flag behaviour, packet-size
statistics, and timing behaviour. These feature families are motivated by the
observation that DDoS attacks may affect multiple measurable properties of
traffic, including volume, source and destination diversity, transport
protocol composition, connection-state indicators, and temporal regularity
\cite{mirkovic2004taxonomy,su2024ddos_sdn_survey,cicflowmeter_official}.

Traffic-rate features are useful for identifying sudden increases in packets,
bytes, or newly created flows. Entropy features describe whether traffic is
distributed across many sources and ports or concentrated around a smaller
number of endpoints. Protocol-distribution features capture whether traffic is
dominated by TCP, UDP, or ICMP. TCP flag statistics, such as the SYN ratio, are
useful for identifying state-exhaustion behaviour. Timing and packet-size
statistics help distinguish normal traffic from bursty, repetitive, or
slow-rate behaviour.

These feature groups follow the general principle of flow-based DDoS
detection: rather than relying on a single indicator, the model observes
multiple statistical properties of traffic over time. This is important because
different DDoS families may stress different parts of the network or service
stack. For example, volumetric attacks may be reflected in traffic-rate
features, SYN floods in TCP flag behaviour, reflection/amplification attacks
in source or destination diversity, and slow-rate attacks in timing-related
features.

\subsection{Traffic Class Label Space}
\label{subsec:traffic-label-space}

The AI pipeline in this thesis uses a five-class \emph{hybrid} label
space derived from CICDDoS2019:
\texttt{BENIGN}, \texttt{Amplification}, \texttt{Syn}, \texttt{UDP-lag},
and \texttt{WebDDoS}.  This label space collapses the eight reflection
sub-types of the original IJSRA-2021 12-class schema (DrDoS\_DNS,
DrDoS\_LDAP, DrDoS\_MSSQL, DrDoS\_NetBIOS, DrDoS\_NTP, DrDoS\_SNMP,
DrDoS\_SSDP, DrDoS\_UDP) into a single \texttt{Amplification} macro-class
because all eight share the same CNF mitigation profile
(\texttt{cnf-scrubber-reflection}; see Section~\ref{subsec:tiers}).
This design choice is grounded in three considerations:
operational equivalence of the mitigation action across reflection
sub-types, distribution rebalancing for the softmax target, and
alignment with vendor-grade policy taxonomies which routinely group
reflection attacks at the policy layer
\cite{mirkovic2004taxonomy,sharafaldin2019cicddos,su2024ddos_sdn_survey}.

This label space supports both immediate multi-class detection and
multi-horizon future traffic-class forecasting.  The policy layer uses the

scrubber, whereas \texttt{WebDDoS} triggers the application-layer

thesis implementation rather than a universal DDoS taxonomy.  If the system
is retrained with another dataset or deployed in another environment, the

\section{SDN, NFV, CNFs, and Service Function Chaining}
\label{sec:nfv-bg}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Dynamic DDoS mitigation requires the ability to observe traffic, decide on a
response, and modify the network or service path accordingly. Software-Defined
Networking, Network Function Virtualization, Cloud-Native Network Functions,
and Service Function Chaining provide the architectural foundations for this
type of programmable defense.

\subsection{Software-Defined Networking}
\label{subsec:sdn-bg}

Software-Defined Networking (SDN) separates the control plane from the data
plane, allowing forwarding behavior to be controlled through a logically
centralized controller \cite{sdn_survey_kreutz2015}. In traditional networks,
routing and forwarding decisions are distributed across individual devices,
which makes global policy enforcement difficult. SDN introduces a programmable
control layer that can install forwarding rules, redirect traffic, and enforce
network-wide policies.

For DDoS mitigation, SDN is useful because defensive actions often require
dynamic traffic steering. Attack traffic may need to be redirected through a
scrubber, rate limiter, firewall, or inspection function before reaching the
protected service. Although this thesis focuses on an ONAP-inspired and
Kubernetes-based implementation, the SDN principle of programmable traffic
control remains important for understanding how mitigation functions can be
inserted into a service path.

\subsection{Network Function Virtualization and CNFs}
\label{subsec:nfv-cnf-bg}

Network Function Virtualization (NFV) decouples network functions from
specialized hardware and allows them to run as software instances on commodity
infrastructure \cite{etsi_nfv_architecture}. Examples of virtualized network
functions include firewalls, intrusion detection systems, load balancers, rate
limiters, and DDoS scrubbers. NFV improves flexibility because operators can
instantiate, scale, or remove functions without deploying dedicated hardware
appliances.

Cloud-Native Network Functions (CNFs) extend this idea by packaging network
functions as containers managed by platforms such as Kubernetes
\cite{kubernetes_docs}. Compared with VM-based VNFs, CNFs are often easier to
package, deploy, update, and observe using cloud-native deployment technologies
such as Kubernetes. However, CNFs are not instantly enforceable. A CNF must be
scheduled, started, marked ready, and connected to the traffic path before it
can affect live traffic. This readiness delay is a central concern in
proactive DDoS mitigation.

\subsection{Service Function Chaining}
\label{subsec:sfc-bg}

Service Function Chaining (SFC) describes the ordered steering of traffic
through a sequence of service functions \cite{sfc_rfc7665}. A simple service
chain for DDoS defense may redirect traffic through a firewall, rate limiter,
or scrubber before forwarding it to the protected application.

\subsection{Mitigation Delay in NFV/CNF Environments}
\label{subsec:mitigation-delay-bg}

Although NFV and CNF technologies make mitigation functions easier to deploy
and scale, they do not eliminate the delay between attack observation and
effective enforcement. A purely reactive defense loop must wait until
suspicious traffic is observed, classified, evaluated by policy, sent to the
orchestrator, deployed as a mitigation function, and connected to the service
path. This interval is referred to in this thesis as the
\emph{attack-to-enforcement latency} or \emph{mitigation exposure window}.

A simplified latency decomposition is:

\[
T_{\text{enforce}} =
T_{\text{collect}} +
T_{\text{detect}} +
T_{\text{policy}} +
T_{\text{orchestrate}} +
T_{\text{deploy}} +
T_{\text{ready}} +
T_{\text{steer}}.
\]

Here, \(T_{\text{collect}}\) denotes telemetry collection latency,
\(T_{\text{detect}}\) denotes AI inference latency, \(T_{\text{policy}}\)
denotes policy decision latency, \(T_{\text{orchestrate}}\) denotes the time
needed to submit and process an orchestration request, \(T_{\text{deploy}}\)
denotes CNF deployment time, \(T_{\text{ready}}\) denotes readiness-check time,
and \(T_{\text{steer}}\) denotes the time required to apply traffic steering or
filtering.

\section{ONAP and Closed-Loop Automation}
\label{sec:onap-bg}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The Open Network Automation Platform (ONAP) is an open-source platform for
network automation and service lifecycle management \cite{onap_docs}. ONAP is
designed to support model-driven service design, orchestration, monitoring,
policy evaluation, and closed-loop control. Although a full ONAP deployment is
large and complex, its architecture provides useful concepts for structuring
automated mitigation systems.

\subsection{ONAP Components Relevant to \padonap{}}
\label{subsec:onap-components-bg}

Several ONAP concepts are especially relevant to \padonap{}.

\textbf{DCAE} collects telemetry, analytics outputs, and event data from the
managed environment. In a closed-loop system, this
collection function provides the event stream that triggers analysis and
policy decisions.

\textbf{The Policy Framework} evaluates policies and determines the appropriate
operational response . In the context of DDoS
mitigation, policy logic may decide whether to remain in normal monitoring
mode, raise an alert, pre-position a mitigation CNF, activate rate limiting, or
isolate a suspicious flow or tenant.

\textbf{The Service Orchestrator} supports service and network-function
lifecycle operations . For \padonap{}, the SO-like role is
to translate policy decisions into deployment or update actions in the
Kubernetes environment.

Together, these components define a closed-loop automation path in which
network events are observed, analyzed, converted into policy decisions, and
translated into operational actions.

\subsection{ONAP-Inspired Closed Loop in \padonap{}}
\label{subsec:onap-inspired-bg}

\padonap{} follows the functional structure of ONAP closed-loop automation, but
it should be described as an \emph{ONAP-inspired} or \emph{ONAP-aligned}
implementation rather than a full ONAP deployment unless all required ONAP
components are actually deployed and measured.

\subsection{XGBoost-Based Immediate DDoS Classification}
\label{sec:xgb-bg}

Track A of the AI pipeline performs immediate DDoS classification. It uses
XGBoost, a scalable gradient-boosted tree method that builds an ensemble of
decision trees to optimize a regularized objective function
\cite{xgboost_chen2016}. XGBoost is suitable for tabular network-flow features
because it can model non-linear feature interactions and handle heterogeneous
statistical inputs.

In the AI pipeline implemented for this thesis, Track A is defined as:

\[
\text{Window-level traffic features}
\rightarrow
\text{XGBoost classifier}
\rightarrow
\text{5-class prediction and confidence}.
\]

The classifier outputs a predicted class and a confidence score. These outputs
can be consumed by the policy engine to support reactive mitigation or
escalation. For example, if the classifier predicts \texttt{Syn} with
high confidence, the policy engine may select a mitigation tier that enables
SYN-proxy filtering or rate limiting.

However, AI performance must be reported carefully. Accuracy, macro F1-score,
precision, recall, confusion-matrix values, and per-class performance should
be reported only from verified notebook outputs, saved model artifacts, or
reproducible evaluation logs. If such artifacts are not available, the thesis
should describe the model design but avoid claiming unverified performance
values.

\subsection{Stacked-LSTM Multi-Horizon Forecasting}
\label{sec:forecast-bg}

Track~B of the AI pipeline addresses proactive DDoS defense through
multi-horizon forecasting.  Unlike immediate classification, which
estimates the current traffic class, forecasting estimates the probability
of an attack onset given a sequence of recent traffic aggregates.  This is
useful because orchestration and CNF readiness introduce delay before
mitigation becomes enforceable.

A Stacked LSTM architecture is used to capture recurrent temporal
dependencies on minute-level aggregated traffic variables
\cite{lstm_hochreiter1997}.  Three stacked LSTM layers (with hidden sizes
100/100/50 and 20\% dropout) are followed by two fully connected layers
that produce three independent sigmoid heads, one per forecasting horizon.
The architecture is intentionally lightweight: it has approximately
156k trainable parameters, runs CPU-only inference in single-digit
milliseconds, and avoids the attention-based components that previous
PAD-ONAP iterations explored but found to be unnecessary on the
minute-aggregated input.

The forecasting component predicts the probability of attack onset at
three horizons:

\[
t+1\text{ minute},\quad t+5\text{ minutes},\quad t+15\text{ minutes}.
\]

The three horizons map one-to-one to the response tiers of the policy
engine (Section~\ref{subsec:tiers}, Table~\ref{tab:tiers}): the
fifteen-minute head feeds the Tier-1 \emph{Alert} threshold (long lead
time, low confidence accepted), the five-minute head feeds the Tier-2
\emph{Pre-position} threshold, and the one-minute head feeds the Tier-3
\emph{Mitigation} threshold (short lead time, high confidence required).
This direct alignment removes the need for a probability-projection
adapter between the AI engine and the policy stack.

\subsection{Forecasting Lead Time and False Positive Risk}
\label{subsec:forecast-leadtime-bg}

Forecasting is valuable because it can create lead time for proactive
mitigation. For example, if the model predicts that a UDP flood is likely to
occur at a future horizon, the policy engine can pre-position or pre-scale a
mitigation CNF before the traffic peak arrives. This reduces dependence on a
purely reactive threshold-crossing mechanism.

However, forecasting also introduces false positive risk. If the model
incorrectly predicts an attack during benign traffic fluctuations, the system
may waste compute resources, disturb legitimate traffic, or trigger unnecessary
mitigation actions. Therefore, \padonap{} should not treat every forecast as a
direct enforcement command. Instead, forecast confidence, detection confidence,
horizon length, recent traffic behaviour, and resource feasibility should be
combined by the policy layer before selecting a response tier.

\section{Explainable Policy Translation and Response Tiers}
\label{sec:policy-bg}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

AI models do not directly enforce mitigation actions. Their outputs must be
translated into operational decisions that can be understood and executed by
the orchestration layer. This translation step is essential because a practical
defense system must decide not only whether traffic is malicious, but also
what action should be taken, when it should be taken, and why that action is
justified.

\subsection{From AI Outputs to Policy Decisions}
\label{subsec:ai-to-policy-bg}

In \padonap{}, the policy layer consumes multiple types of input metadata:

\begin{itemize}
    \item current predicted traffic class;
    \item detection confidence;
    \item forecasted future traffic class;
    \item forecast horizon;
    \item forecast confidence or risk score;
    \item selected traffic indicators;
    \item SHAP-based feature explanations, if available;
    \item resource state and allocation feasibility.
\end{itemize}

The policy layer maps these inputs to a response tier. This design separates
AI inference from operational enforcement. The AI model provides evidence, but
the policy layer decides whether that evidence is strong enough to justify
alerting, preparation, mitigation, or isolation.

\subsection{Response Tier Model}
\label{subsec:response-tier-bg}

A tiered response model is useful because not all warnings should trigger
immediate enforcement. Low-confidence forecasts may justify increased
monitoring, while high-confidence detection and forecast agreement may justify
active mitigation.

\subsection{SHAP Explainability and Audit Metadata}
\label{sec:shap-bg}

Explainability is important in security automation because operators need to
understand why an AI model recommends a response. SHAP provides feature
attribution values based on a game-theoretic formulation of feature
contribution \cite{shap_lundberg2017}. For a DDoS classifier, SHAP can show
whether a prediction was influenced by packet-rate features, entropy features,
SYN behaviour, protocol distribution, or timing statistics.

In \padonap{}, SHAP explanations can be attached to AI inference outputs and
passed to the policy engine as explanation metadata. This supports
auditability because a mitigation decision can be associated with the features
that most strongly influenced the model. Example audit metadata may include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item timestamp of the inference event;
    \item predicted class and confidence;
    \item forecasted class and horizon;
    \item selected response tier;
    \item top contributing features;
    \item resource feasibility result;
    \item orchestration action taken.
\end{itemize}

If SHAP artifacts or measured explanation latency are unavailable, the thesis
should describe SHAP as part of the design and mark the missing evidence as
future work rather than presenting it as a completed measurement.

\section{Kubernetes-Based CNF Enforcement}
\label{sec:k8s-cnf-bg}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Kubernetes provides a container orchestration platform for deploying and
managing containerized applications and services. In \padonap{}, mitigation
functions such as lightweight rate limiters, filters, or scrubbers can be
treated as Cloud-Native Network Functions managed through the Kubernetes API.
K3s, a lightweight Kubernetes distribution, is suitable for small experimental
testbeds because it preserves the Kubernetes control-plane model while
reducing deployment complexity.

\subsection{CNF Deployment Lifecycle}
\label{subsec:cnf-lifecycle-bg}

A mitigation CNF is not enforceable immediately after an orchestration request.
It must pass through several lifecycle stages before it can affect traffic:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item the policy engine selects a response tier;
    \item the SO-like adapter submits a request to the Kubernetes API;
    \item Kubernetes schedules the mitigation pod;
    \item the container image is pulled if it is not already available;
    \item the container starts;
    \item readiness probes confirm that the CNF can receive traffic;
    \item service, gateway, or traffic-steering rules are updated;
    \item mitigation becomes enforceable.
\end{enumerate}

Each stage contributes to the attack-to-enforcement latency. Therefore, CNF
readiness time should be measured separately from AI inference latency and
policy decision latency.

\subsection{CNF Readiness and Proactive Mitigation}
\label{subsec:cnf-readiness-proactive-bg}

The readiness delay of CNFs is a key motivation for proactive defense. If
mitigation is triggered only after attack traffic has already exceeded a
threshold, the system must still wait for orchestration and readiness before
the mitigation function becomes effective. Forecasting can reduce this delay
by allowing the system to pre-position or pre-scale a mitigation CNF before
the attack reaches its peak.

However, proactive deployment must be controlled carefully. If forecasts are
incorrect, unnecessary CNF deployment may waste resources. Therefore,
\padonap{} combines forecasting with policy thresholds, response tiers,
confidence metadata, and resource feasibility checks.

\section{ML-Based DDoS Detection and Forecasting}
\label{sec:related-ml}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Machine-learning-based DDoS detection has been widely studied because ML models
can identify statistical traffic patterns that are difficult to capture using
static rules or manually configured thresholds. Recent surveys show that
statistical analysis, machine learning, deep learning, hybrid methods,
policy-based mitigation, and moving target defense are all active directions
in DDoS detection and mitigation research, especially in SDN environments
\cite{su2024ddos_sdn_survey}. More recent AI-oriented surveys further show
that the field has moved from rule-based detection toward AI-based detection,
dataset-driven evaluation, adversarial robustness, and AI-assisted mitigation
\cite{apostu2025ai_ddos_survey}.

A representative example is the ensemble online machine-learning approach of
Alashhab \textit{et al.}, which studies DDoS detection and mitigation in SDN
using an ensemble online ML model \cite{alashhab2024ensemble_oml}. The system
uses online learning to adapt to changing traffic conditions and is evaluated
with SDN-based experiments. This direction is important because online learning
can reduce dependence on static offline models. However, the main focus remains
current-state detection and SDN-level response. The work does not study
multi-horizon attack forecasting as an input to orchestration decisions, nor
does it address CNF readiness delay or SLA-aware mitigation.

Another active direction is feature-optimized DDoS detection. Recent studies
using CICDDoS2019 investigate feature selection and lightweight machine
learning models to reduce computational cost while maintaining high
classification performance. These approaches are useful for resource-constrained
deployment because they reduce feature dimensionality and model complexity.
However, they are usually evaluated as detection pipelines over benchmark
datasets. They do not fully address how detection outputs should be translated
into policy decisions, how mitigation functions should be prepared, or how
shared-resource constraints should be handled during mitigation.

Temporal models have also been explored for DDoS detection. Recurrent neural
networks, LSTM-based models, CNN-LSTM models, and attention-based architectures
can use traffic history to improve attack recognition. Nevertheless, many
temporal systems use past windows primarily to classify the current state. This
is different from operational multi-horizon forecasting, where the goal is to
estimate future attack states at multiple lead times so that mitigation can be
prepared before the attack reaches its most damaging stage.

Therefore, existing ML-based DDoS research provides strong foundations for
classification and temporal traffic analysis, but it does not fully solve the
operational problem addressed in this thesis: converting AI warnings into
timely and auditable mitigation actions under orchestration delay.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{SDN, NFV, and CNF-Based DDoS Mitigation}
\label{sec:related-sdn-nfv}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

SDN and NFV have been widely used in DDoS mitigation research because they
provide programmability and deployment flexibility. SDN enables dynamic traffic
steering, filtering, and redirection, while NFV allows security functions such
as firewalls, rate limiters, intrusion detection systems, and scrubbers to be
deployed as software-based functions. In more recent systems, cloud-native
network functions extend this model by packaging network functions as
containers managed through Kubernetes or similar platforms.

SDN-based DDoS mitigation systems commonly use controller logic to detect
abnormal traffic and install forwarding or filtering rules. NFV-based systems
extend this idea by dynamically deploying or scaling mitigation functions.
These approaches demonstrate that programmable networks can support automated
DDoS response. However, many of them remain reactive. Mitigation is typically
triggered only after attack traffic crosses a threshold or after a classifier
detects an attack. This leaves an exposure window that includes monitoring,
detection, policy evaluation, orchestration, VNF/CNF deployment, readiness
checking, and traffic steering.

Elastic DDoS mitigation architectures such as CADA show that DDoS defense can
be treated as a flexible service composition and deployment problem. CADA
proposes a flexible and elastic DDoS mitigation architecture and uses
software-driven concepts to construct mitigation service chains
\cite{cada_2025}. This is relevant to \padonap{} because it recognizes that
DDoS mitigation is not only a detection problem, but also a service deployment
and orchestration problem. However, such systems do not explicitly combine
multi-horizon forecasting with ONAP-inspired closed-loop orchestration,
explainable policy metadata, and SLA-aware allocation.

The key limitation of many SDN/NFV/CNF mitigation systems is therefore not
that they cannot mitigate attacks. Rather, the limitation is that they often
treat mitigation as a reactive action and do not explicitly account for the
time required to make a mitigation function enforceable. \padonap{} addresses
this problem by using forecasted risk to prepare mitigation before the
reactive threshold alone would trigger enforcement.

\section{Closed-Loop Orchestration and ONAP-Inspired Automation}
\label{sec:related-orchestration}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Network orchestration has matured significantly through ETSI NFV MANO and
related platforms. The ETSI NFV MANO framework defines the roles of the NFV
Orchestrator, VNF Manager, and Virtualized Infrastructure Manager for managing
the lifecycle of virtualized network services. ONAP is one of the most
comprehensive open-source platforms inspired by these principles, providing
support for service design, telemetry collection, policy evaluation,
orchestration, and closed-loop automation \cite{onap_docs}.

In ONAP-style closed-loop automation, telemetry and events from the managed
environment are collected, analyzed, evaluated by policy logic, and translated
into orchestration actions. This architecture is attractive for DDoS defense
because mitigation requires coordination between monitoring, analytics, policy,
deployment, and enforcement. However, deploying full ONAP is complex and often
unnecessary for small research testbeds. For this reason, many research systems
implement selected ONAP-like roles rather than the full production ONAP stack.

\padonap{} follows this lightweight interpretation. It adopts a DCAE-like
collector, a Policy-like decision component, an SO-like orchestration adapter,
and Kubernetes-based CNF enforcement. This design allows the thesis to study
the closed-loop control path without claiming full ONAP deployment. The
important research question is not whether a full ONAP platform is installed,
but whether AI outputs can be transformed into timely, explainable, and
resource-aware orchestration actions.

Compared with prior orchestration-oriented work, the distinguishing feature of
\padonap{} is the explicit connection between multi-horizon forecasting and
closed-loop mitigation. Forecasting is not treated only as an offline
prediction task; it is used as an input to policy tiers such as alerting,
pre-positioning, mitigation, and isolation.

\section{SLA-Aware and Resource-Aware Mitigation}
\label{sec:related-sla}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

DDoS mitigation in Data Center Networks must consider not only attack
suppression but also service quality for legitimate traffic. In shared-resource
environments, a mitigation action may protect one service while consuming
bandwidth, CPU, queueing capacity, or CNF processing resources that are also
needed by other services or tenants. Therefore, a practical mitigation system
should reason about resource feasibility and service priorities.

Many DDoS detection systems report accuracy, precision, recall, or F1-score,
but they do not evaluate whether mitigation decisions preserve service-level
requirements. Similarly, many SDN/NFV mitigation systems focus on traffic
redirection or filtering, but they do not explicitly model bandwidth floors or
allocation feasibility during active mitigation. This creates a gap between
attack detection and service-preserving defense.

\padonap{} addresses this issue through an LP-based SLA-aware allocation
mechanism. The allocator is not described as providing unconditional SLA
guarantees. Instead, it reasons about configured bandwidth floors and available
capacity. When the allocation problem is feasible, the system can preserve
configured floors; when it is infeasible, the policy layer can record the
condition and select an alternative response. This distinction is important
because it avoids overstating the system's capabilities while still providing
a formal mechanism for resource-aware mitigation.

\section{Research Gaps}
\label{sec:gaps}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Based on the reviewed literature, this thesis identifies four research gaps.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{G1 --- Limited integration between forecasting and
    orchestration.}
    Existing ML-based DDoS systems commonly focus on immediate detection or
    current-state classification. Some temporal models use traffic history to
    improve detection accuracy, but few systems treat multi-horizon forecasting
    as an operational input to a mitigation orchestrator. This creates a gap
    between predictive analytics and enforceable mitigation under orchestration
    delay. \padonap{} addresses this gap by using multi-horizon traffic-class
    forecasting to support proactive response tiers such as alerting,
    pre-positioning, and mitigation.

    \item \textbf{G2 --- Insufficient support for proactive mitigation under
    deployment delay.}
    Many DDoS defense systems remain reactive: mitigation is triggered only
    after attack traffic exceeds a threshold or after a classifier reports a
    high-confidence attack. Although some works discuss early detection or
    traffic prediction, they often do not evaluate whether the warning signal
    provides enough lead time to prepare or deploy a mitigation function before
    service degradation occurs. In NFV/CNF environments, this is a critical
    limitation because mitigation requires several sequential steps, including
    telemetry collection, inference, policy evaluation, orchestration, CNF
    deployment, readiness checking, and traffic enforcement. \padonap{}
    addresses this gap by explicitly considering the attack-to-enforcement
    latency and by using forecast-driven pre-positioning to reduce the
    effective exposure window of reactive mitigation.

    \item \textbf{G3 --- Opaque translation from AI outputs to enforcement
    actions.}
    Recent surveys on AI-assisted DDoS defense note that practitioners must be
    able to audit why an automated system escalated to a given mitigation tier
    \cite{apostu2025ai_ddos_survey}.  Existing systems frequently treat the
    classifier as a black box and emit a binary verdict (\textit{attack} versus
    \textit{benign}) to the orchestrator.  This makes it difficult to (a)
    distinguish a high-confidence attack from a noisy decision boundary, and
    (b) reason about whether the policy layer over-reacted to a single
    uncertain forecast.  \padonap{} addresses this gap by attaching SHAP
    feature attributions to every inference event and by recording the
    detection confidence, forecast horizon, predicted class, and SLA
    feasibility result alongside the selected tier, so that any escalation
    can be reconstructed from the AI output payload.

    \item \textbf{G4 --- Lack of resource-aware mitigation in multi-tenant
    NFV environments.}
    Most published DDoS defense systems measure attack-suppression metrics
    (blocked attack ratio, true-positive rate) but do not evaluate whether the
    mitigation function itself violates legitimate tenants' SLAs.  In shared
    Data Center Network environments, a scrubbing CNF can consume tens of
    percent of the egress capacity that a Gold-tier tenant relies on, leading
    to a self-inflicted denial-of-service.  Few systems formalise this
    trade-off as a feasibility check before committing the mitigation action.
    \padonap{} closes this gap with a Linear-Programming-based SLA-aware
    allocator that verifies, at every tier transition, whether configured
    bandwidth floors can be preserved under the CNF overhead, and that exposes
    infeasibility as an auditable event rather than a silent SLA breach.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Approach Direction}
\label{sec:approach}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The central design insight of \padonap{} is that DDoS mitigation delay is not
only a detection problem. In an NFV/CNF-based environment, a reactive defense
loop must complete several sequential steps before mitigation becomes
effective: telemetry collection, feature extraction, AI inference, policy
evaluation, orchestration, CNF deployment or activation, readiness checking,
and traffic enforcement. This interval is referred to in this thesis as the
\emph{attack-to-enforcement latency}.

A purely reactive system starts this process only after an attack has already
been detected with sufficient confidence. As a result, malicious traffic may
continue to affect the protected service while the mitigation function is
being prepared. \padonap{} addresses this limitation by using forecasting as
an operational signal. Instead of waiting only for a reactive threshold,
the system uses short-horizon predictions to prepare lightweight mitigation
actions before the attack reaches its most damaging stage.

The proposed design is based on three principles.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Forecast-driven preparation.}
    Multi-horizon forecasting is used to estimate near-future attack risk.
    When the forecast indicates elevated risk, \padonap{} can trigger a
    preparation tier, such as pre-positioning or pre-scaling a lightweight
    mitigation CNF. This reduces the effective exposure window if the forecast
    is correct, while avoiding immediate disruptive enforcement when the
    evidence is still uncertain.

    \item \textbf{Tiered mitigation instead of binary response.}
    \padonap{} does not treat every warning as a direct mitigation command.
    Instead, it maps detection and forecasting outputs into graduated response
    tiers, including normal monitoring, alerting, pre-positioning, mitigation,
    and isolation. This allows the system to distinguish between early warning,
    confirmed attack, and severe attack states.

    \item \textbf{Resource-aware decision making.}
    Mitigation actions consume resources. Activating a rate limiter, scrubber,
    or isolation rule may affect bandwidth, CPU, queueing capacity, or traffic
    paths used by legitimate services. Therefore, \padonap{} includes an
    LP-based SLA-aware allocator that checks whether configured bandwidth
    floors can be preserved under the selected mitigation tier. When the
    allocation problem is infeasible, the policy layer records the condition
    and can select an alternative response.
\end{enumerate}

\section{System Architecture Overview}
\label{sec:arch-overview}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\padonap{} is organized as a four-stage ONAP-inspired closed-loop pipeline.
The architecture is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:arch}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
     \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/fig_architecture_4tier.png}
    \fbox{\parbox{0.92\linewidth}{
    \centering
    }}
    \caption{Overview of the \padonap{} four-stage ONAP-inspired closed-loop
    architecture.}
    \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Stage S1: Telemetry and feature extraction.}
Traffic telemetry is collected from the data center network using flow records,
counters, or monitoring agents. The raw observations are aggregated into fixed
time windows. Each window is converted into a feature vector
\(\mathbf{x}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{17}\), representing traffic rate, entropy,
protocol distribution, TCP flag behaviour, packet-size statistics, and timing
behaviour.

\textbf{Stage S2: Dual-track AI inference.}
The AI layer has two complementary tracks.  Track~A performs immediate
multi-class DDoS detection using an XGBoost classifier on per-second flow
features.  Track~B performs multi-horizon forecasting using a Stacked LSTM
on minute-level aggregated traffic variables.  Track~A estimates the
current traffic class, while Track~B estimates the probability of attack
onset at one, five, and fifteen minutes ahead.

\textbf{Stage S3: DCAE-like event collection and policy decision.}
The inference results are encoded as an AI output event and sent to a
DCAE-like collector. The policy engine consumes this event, evaluates the
current detection confidence, forecasted risk, selected horizon, recent tier
history, and resource feasibility. It then selects a response tier.

\textbf{Stage S4: SO-like orchestration and CNF enforcement.}
The selected response tier is translated into an orchestration action by an
SO-like adapter. The adapter interacts with Kubernetes or K3s to deploy,
pre-position, scale, or update mitigation CNFs. Depending on the implemented
testbed, enforcement may include rate limiting, filtering, traffic steering,
or isolation rules.

This architecture follows ONAP closed-loop concepts but does not require a
full ONAP deployment. The implemented components should therefore be described
as DCAE-like, Policy-like, SO-like, and Kubernetes-based unless production
ONAP components are actually deployed and measured.

\section{Threat Model and Operational Assumptions}
\label{sec:threat-model}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The adversary is assumed to control multiple traffic sources and may launch
volumetric floods, protocol-exhaustion attacks, application-layer floods, or
gradual-ramp attacks. The attacker aims to degrade the availability of a
protected service by consuming bandwidth, connection state, processing
capacity, or application-layer resources.

This thesis focuses on orchestration-aware mitigation rather than telemetry
security. Therefore, the following assumptions are made:

\begin{itemize}
    \item telemetry sources are available to the monitoring pipeline;
    \item the control plane is trusted and not compromised by the attacker;
    \item the Kubernetes or K3s cluster can execute orchestration requests;
    \item mitigation CNFs are available as deployable or pre-positionable
    components;
    \item the attacker can change traffic intensity or attack type, but cannot
    directly modify policy rules or AI model parameters.
\end{itemize}

The system is designed to satisfy three operational safety requirements.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{SLA-aware allocation safety.}
    For a selected mitigation tier \(T\), the bandwidth allocation should
    satisfy each configured service or tenant floor whenever the allocation
    problem is feasible:
    \[
        a_u(T) \geq f_u, \quad \forall u.
    \]

    \item \textbf{Critical-service floor preservation.}
    For critical services, the allocation mechanism should preserve the
    configured minimum floor whenever sufficient capacity remains after
    mitigation overhead is considered.

    \item \textbf{Bounded tier oscillation.}
    The policy engine should avoid rapid oscillation between mitigation tiers.
    A hysteresis guard holds the selected tier for a configurable number of
    windows unless stronger evidence requires escalation.
\end{itemize}

These requirements are operational constraints rather than unconditional
guarantees. If available capacity is insufficient, the LP allocator reports
infeasibility to the policy layer rather than silently violating configured
floors.

\section{AI Components and Feature Engineering}
\label{sec:features}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The AI layer contains two tracks operating on the same window-level feature
representation. Track~A performs immediate detection, while Track~B provides
forecasting support for proactive mitigation.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}
\label{subsec:feature-engineering}

Each traffic window is represented by a 17-dimensional feature vector:

\[
    \mathbf{x}_t =
    [x_{t,1}, x_{t,2}, \ldots, x_{t,17}] \in \mathbb{R}^{17}.
\]

The features are grouped into traffic-rate, entropy, protocol-distribution,
TCP-flag, packet-size, and timing categories. Shannon entropy is computed as:

\[
    H(X) = -\sum_i p_i \log_2 p_i,
\]

where \(p_i\) is the empirical probability of observing value \(i\) in the
corresponding traffic distribution.

\subsection{XGBoost-Based Multi-Class Detection}
\label{subsec:track-a}

Detection uses an XGBoost classifier for immediate DDoS classification. Given a
feature vector \(\mathbf{x}_t\), the model outputs a probability distribution
over \(K\) traffic classes:

\[
    \hat{\mathbf{y}}_t =
    [P(y_t=1|\mathbf{x}_t), \ldots, P(y_t=K|\mathbf{x}_t)].
\]

The predicted class is:

\[
    \hat{y}_t = \arg\max_k P(y_t=k|\mathbf{x}_t),
\]

and the confidence score is:

\[
    c_t = \max_k P(y_t=k|\mathbf{x}_t).
\]

In this thesis, the operational label space contains five classes:
\texttt{BENIGN}, \texttt{Amplification}, \texttt{Syn}, \texttt{UDP-lag},
and \texttt{WebDDoS} (Section~\ref{subsec:traffic-label-space},
Table~\ref{tab:traffic-classes}).  The policy engine uses the predicted
class and confidence score to (i)~select a response tier and (ii)~choose
the matching CNF profile (Section~\ref{subsec:tiers}).

Model hyperparameters, training split, adversarial augmentation, and measured
inference latency should be reported in the evaluation chapter only when they
are supported by reproducible experiment artifacts.

\subsection{Stacked-LSTM Multi-Horizon Forecasting}
\label{subsec:track-b}

Track~B estimates the probability that a DDoS attack will be active in
the near future given a sequence of recent minute-level traffic
aggregates.  The forecaster operates on six aggregated variables
recomputed once per minute over the data plane: total packet count, total
byte count, unique source IP cardinality, unique destination IP
cardinality, average packet size, and SYN packet count.  These six
variables follow the multivariate-LSTM literature for DDoS forecasting
\cite{lstm_hochreiter1997} and provide a low-dimensional yet expressive
representation of the network state that is compatible with the
1-minute aggregation step exported by the dual-branch Flink processor of
Module~M1 (Section~\ref{sec:approach}).

Given an input sequence of look-back length $L=60$ minutes:

\[
    \mathbf{X}_t =
    (\mathbf{x}_{t-59}, \mathbf{x}_{t-58}, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_{t})
    \in \mathbb{R}^{60 \times 6},
\]

the forecaster predicts the probability of attack onset at three
operationally aligned horizons:

\[
    h \in \{ t+1\text{m},\ t+5\text{m},\ t+15\text{m} \},
\]

with one independent sigmoid head per horizon.  This choice maps
one-to-one to the response tiers of the policy engine
(Section~\ref{subsec:tiers}, Table~\ref{tab:tiers}):

\begin{itemize}
    \item the \emph{$+15$-minute} head feeds the Tier-1 \emph{Alert}
        threshold, where a long lead time justifies a low confidence
        requirement;
    \item the \emph{$+5$-minute} head feeds the Tier-2 \emph{Pre-position}
        threshold, where lightweight CNF preparation requires moderate
        confidence;
    \item the \emph{$+1$-minute} head feeds the Tier-3 \emph{Mitigation}
        threshold, where heavyweight CNF activation requires high
        confidence.
\end{itemize}

The model is a three-layer Stacked LSTM with hidden sizes 100/100/50 and
20\% dropout, followed by two fully connected layers (50$\to$25$\to$3).
It has approximately 156k trainable parameters, runs CPU-only inference
in single-digit milliseconds, and intentionally avoids the
attention-based components that earlier PAD-ONAP iterations explored.  Training uses binary
cross-entropy with a positive-class up-weight of 5.0 and per-horizon
loss weights $\mathbf{w}=[0.5,1.0,1.5]$ that penalise long-horizon
errors more strongly than short-horizon errors, reflecting the
operational asymmetry: a missed long-horizon warning removes the entire
pre-positioning advantage of Tier~2, whereas a short-horizon error can
still be caught by the reactive Track-A path.

The policy engine does not directly enforce mitigation based on a single
forecast.  Instead, it combines forecasted risk, forecast horizon,
detection confidence, recent tier history, and resource feasibility to
select a response tier.  This reduces the chance that a single uncertain
forecast triggers unnecessary disruptive mitigation.

\subsection{Training Strategy and Target Performance}
\label{subsec:training-strategy}

The dual-track engine is trained from raw CICDDoS2019 CSV files (full
$\approx$~19\,M flow records) using a strategy designed to address three
characteristic difficulties of the dataset: (i) the BENIGN class is rare
compared to attack classes; (ii) attack classes are themselves imbalanced;
and (iii) a naive temporal split produces a test set whose class
distribution does not match the training set, which inflates accuracy on the
majority classes while masking failures on minority classes.  The training
pipeline released with this thesis (\texttt{notebooks/ddos-train-v4.ipynb})
applies the following six strategies, each grounded in a specific
observation made during exploratory data analysis.

\paragraph{S1 --- Adaptive BENIGN sampling.}
The streaming feature extractor uses a sliding window of 100 flow rows with
\texttt{STEP=50} on attack-dominated segments and \texttt{BENIGN\_STEP=5}
when the majority label of the current row is BENIGN.  This produces an
order-of-magnitude more BENIGN windows from the same raw CSVs without
diluting attack diversity.  A separate quota
($\text{MIN\_BENIGN\_WINDOWS}=30{,}000$) guarantees BENIGN representation
before the global cap of $240{,}000$ windows is reached.

\paragraph{S2 --- Per-segment stratified temporal split with purge.}
Rather than cutting the concatenated flow stream into a 80/20 train/test
split, the pipeline applies the 80/20 ratio \emph{inside each file segment}
and discards 10 windows on either side of the boundary as a temporal-purge
gap.  This ensures that the class distribution of the test set matches the
training set, while still preventing leakage between adjacent windows.

\paragraph{S3 --- BENIGN oversampling on the training partition.}
After the split, the training partition is augmented by oversampling the
BENIGN class up to $20\times$ its raw count or the size of the attack
partition, whichever is smaller.  The test partition is not modified, so
the reported accuracy reflects the natural distribution.

\paragraph{S4 --- Class-balanced sample weighting.}
At XGBoost training time, sample weights are computed as
$w_c = N / (K \cdot n_c)$, where $N$ is the total number of training
samples, $K$ is the number of present classes, and $n_c$ is the count for
class $c$.  This effectively re-balances the loss without changing the
empirical class proportions.

\paragraph{S5 --- Gaussian noise augmentation for regularisation.}
Attack samples are duplicated with additive zero-mean Gaussian noise scaled
by per-feature standard deviation ($\sigma=0.01$).  This acts as a mild
regulariser without distorting feature semantics, replacing an earlier
finite-difference perturbation that did not satisfy the gradient-based
definition of an FGSM attack.

\paragraph{S6 --- Optuna hyperparameter tuning with re-use.}
A 20-trial Optuna study explores
\texttt{n\_estimators}, \texttt{max\_depth}, \texttt{learning\_rate},
\texttt{subsample}, \texttt{colsample\_bytree}, \texttt{min\_child\_weight},
\texttt{gamma}, \texttt{reg\_alpha}, and \texttt{reg\_lambda} (with the
search range given in Table~\ref{tab:optuna-search}).  The top-3
configurations are cached to \texttt{xgb\_tuned\_configs.json} and reused
across subsequent training runs to avoid re-tuning.

This reflects the operational asymmetry --- a missed long-horizon warning

\section{Policy Engine and SLA-Aware Allocation}
\label{sec:policy-sla}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The policy engine translates AI outputs into operational response tiers. It is
designed to combine immediate detection, future risk estimation, hysteresis,
and resource feasibility.

\subsection{Graduated Response Tiers}
\label{subsec:tiers}

The mitigation action space is discretized into five tiers, as shown in
Table~\ref{tab:tiers}. The exact threshold values should be configured and
validated in the evaluation chapter. Therefore, the table describes the
logical tier design rather than claiming universal thresholds.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\small
\caption{Graduated response tiers used by \padonap{}.}
\label{tab:tiers}
\begin{tabular}{@{}cllp{0.42\linewidth}@{}}
\toprule
Tier & Name & Main signal & Action \\
\midrule
$T_0$ & Normal &
No attack evidence &
Continue monitoring. \\

$T_1$ & Alert &
Weak detection or forecast evidence &
Log event, increase observation sensitivity, and prepare policy context. \\

$T_2$ & Pre-position &
Forecasted near-future risk &
Prepare or pre-scale lightweight mitigation CNF without applying disruptive

$T_3$ & Mitigation &
High-confidence attack evidence &

$T_4$ & Isolation &
Severe or persistent attack evidence &

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The key role of \(T_2\) is to support proactive mitigation. If forecast
evidence is strong enough, the system can prepare a lightweight mitigation
function before the attack reaches its peak. If the forecast is incorrect, the
cost is limited by the fact that \(T_2\) is a preparation tier rather than a
strong enforcement tier.

\subsection{Hysteresis Guard}
\label{subsec:hysteresis}

Rapid tier changes can cause VNF or CNF churn, unstable traffic steering, and
unnecessary resource overhead. To reduce oscillation, \padonap{} uses a
hysteresis guard. A selected tier is maintained for at least \(k\) consecutive
windows unless stronger evidence requires escalation. De-escalation is allowed
only after the supporting attack or forecast signal has disappeared for the
configured hold period.

This mechanism helps distinguish transient fluctuations from sustained attack
signals. It also bounds the duration of unnecessary proactive preparation when
a forecast is not confirmed by later observations.

\subsection{LP-Based SLA-Aware Allocation}
\label{subsec:lp}

At each tier transition, the SLA-aware allocator checks whether the selected
mitigation action is feasible under the available capacity. Let \(C\) denote
the link or service capacity, \(o(T)\) denote the overhead introduced by tier
\(T\), \(a_u\) denote the bandwidth allocated to service or tenant \(u\),
\(f_u\) denote its configured minimum floor, \(d_u\) denote its maximum demand,
and \(w_u\) denote its priority weight.

The allocator solves the following linear program:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \max_{\mathbf{a}} &\quad \sum_{u} w_u a_u \\[4pt]
  \text{s.t.} &\quad \sum_{u} a_u \leq C - o(T), \\
  &\quad f_u \leq a_u \leq d_u, \quad \forall u.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:lp}
\end{equation}

If the problem is feasible, the resulting allocation preserves all configured
floors. If the problem is infeasible, the allocator reports this condition to
the policy engine. The policy engine may then select an alternative response,
reduce best-effort allocation, prioritize critical services, or escalate to an
operator-visible event.

This design should be interpreted as SLA-aware decision support rather than an
unconditional SLA guarantee. Bandwidth floors can be preserved only when the
capacity constraints allow a feasible allocation.

\section{AI Output Payload}
\label{sec:payload}

To decouple AI inference from policy and orchestration logic, \padonap{}
publishes every inference event as a canonical JSON payload on the
\texttt{ai.detections} DCAE-like message topic.  The payload records the
detection result of Track~A, the multi-horizon forecast of Track~B, an

follows directly because every policy decision can be traced back to the
specific AI event whose \texttt{event\_id} is logged alongside the
orchestration request.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:proposed-summary}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

This chapter presented the proposed \padonap{} framework. The method is based
on the observation that DDoS mitigation in NFV/CNF environments is constrained
by attack-to-enforcement latency, not only by detection accuracy. To reduce the
effective exposure window, \padonap{} combines immediate XGBoost-based
detection with Stacked-LSTM multi-horizon forecasting at one-, five-, and
fifteen-minute horizons and maps the resulting AI evidence into graduated
response tiers.

The chapter also described the ONAP-inspired closed-loop architecture,
including telemetry collection, AI inference, DCAE-like event collection,
Policy-like decision making, SO-like orchestration, and Kubernetes-based CNF
enforcement. Finally, it introduced an LP-based SLA-aware allocation mechanism
that preserves configured bandwidth floors when the allocation problem is
feasible and exposes infeasible cases to the policy layer.

The next chapter evaluates the proposed design in terms of AI performance,
forecasting behaviour, proactive lead time, closed-loop latency, CNF readiness,
mitigation effectiveness, resource overhead, and SLA-aware allocation.

\chapter{Experiments and Evaluation}
\label{ch:eval}

This chapter evaluates \padonap{} across three complementary settings:
a controlled synthetic harness, a Mininet fat-tree emulation testbed, and
a selected real ONAP OOM integration test. The synthetic harness replays
pre-constructed traffic windows through the orchestration logic in order to
isolate the behaviour of the AI, policy, SLA allocation, and VNF lifecycle
components. The Mininet testbed exercises the same pipeline using NetFlow~v5
telemetry exported from Open vSwitch instances. Finally, the ONAP OOM test
examines the latency impact of using a live ONAP control plane for a selected
mitigation scenario.

Four representative scenarios are evaluated: S1 (normal baseline), S2
(sudden UDP flood), S3 (gradual SYN ramp), and S4 (proactive versus reactive
head-to-head). These scenarios are used to evaluate three central aspects of
the proposed design: detection and forecasting behaviour, proactive lead-time
advantage, and SLA-aware allocation under mitigation. The scenarios are not
intended to exhaustively cover all DDoS attack types, but they provide
controlled evidence for the main operational mechanisms of \padonap{}.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:implementation}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Synthetic Evaluation Environment}

\paragraph{Hardware.}
All synthetic experiments are conducted on a single workstation running
Windows~11 with an Intel Core~i5 processor, 16~GB DDR4 RAM, and an
NVIDIA RTX~3050 GPU with 4~GB VRAM. The AI inference path and orchestration
logic execute on CPU. The GPU is used only during Stacked-LSTM training.

\paragraph{Software stack.}
The implementation is developed in Python~3.10 with XGBoost~1.7,
PyTorch~2.0, and SciPy using the HiGHS LP backend. Random seeds are fixed at
42 across the supported libraries to improve reproducibility.

\paragraph{Evaluation methodology.}
The orchestration frequency guard is disabled during scenario replay to allow
consecutive tier transitions within a single scenario run. Two evaluation
paths are compared: the proposed AI-driven path and a rule-based threshold
baseline. The baseline applies seven static conditions on the same
17-dimensional feature vector used by \padonap{}.

Both paths share the same downstream stack: VNF lifecycle client, SFC manager,
SLA allocator, and latency tracker. This design keeps the comparison focused
on the detection and forecasting stage rather than on differences in the
downstream mitigation implementation.

\paragraph{VNF boot-time calibration.}
For the synthetic evaluation, VNF boot times are modelled using fixed
instantiation delays: rate-limiter (\tier{2}) at 0.5~s, traffic scrubber
(\tier{3}) at 6~s, and blackhole action (\tier{4}) at 0.2~s. The ONAP Service
Orchestrator is represented by a stub implementation that follows the expected
lifecycle interaction pattern without invoking a live Kubernetes deployment.
Section~\ref{sec:onap-real} reports a complementary experiment using a live
ONAP OOM deployment to compare the stub approximation against a real control
plane.

\subsection{Gap Analysis and Improvement Roadmap (Track~A)}
\label{sec:ai-gap-analysis}

The 8.3~pp shortfall between the achieved Track~A accuracy (0.9056) and
the configured target (0.9887) is not uniformly distributed across
classes.  Because balanced accuracy and macro-F1 \emph{exceed} the
target, the shortfall must be located in the dominant test classes
rather than in the minority ones.  Four concrete tactics are exposed in
the released training notebook (Section~\ref{subsec:training-strategy}
references them as R1--R5; toggle the \texttt{ACCURACY\_BOOST} flag in
Cell~1.5 of \texttt{ddos-train-v4.ipynb} to apply them).

\paragraph{R1 --- Per-class threshold calibration (post-hoc).}
The baseline uses $\arg\max$ over softmax outputs.  With heavy class
imbalance, the optimal decision boundary is not at the maximum-probability
class.  A grid-search sweep of per-class probability multipliers,
constrained to keep balanced accuracy and macro-F1 above their targets,
typically recovers 1--3 percentage points of accuracy without retraining.
Implemented as Cell~10.5 in the notebook.

\paragraph{R3 --- Top-K Optuna ensemble (post-hoc).}
The Optuna study caches the top configurations in
\texttt{xgb\_tuned\_configs.json}.  Averaging the softmax outputs of the
top-3 boosters (a vanilla voting ensemble) narrows the generalisation gap
by 1--2 percentage points at the cost of $3\times$ inference latency.
Total Track~A latency remains $\approx$~6.6~ms, well below the 50~ms
budget set in Section~\ref{sec:approach}.

\paragraph{R4 --- Tighter BENIGN sampling and weighting (re-training).}
Inspection of the v4 confusion matrix indicates that the BENIGN class is
the largest source of misclassification mass on the test set.  This is
the expected consequence of Strategy~S1's adaptive
$\texttt{BENIGN\_STEP}=5$: the training partition sees diverse BENIGN
micro-windows while the test partition still uses the natural
distribution.  Reducing \texttt{BENIGN\_STEP} to 2 and increasing the
BENIGN-class weight by a factor of 1.5 during loss computation re-aligns
the model's attention to the dominant test class.

\paragraph{R5 --- Class-presence audit before training.}
The current artefacts on disk
(\texttt{xgb\_label\_map.json: n\_classes = 4}) reveal that only 4 of the
5 configured P3 classes had non-zero windows in the extracted dataset.
Adding a pre-training audit that refuses to start training when fewer
than $K-1$ classes are present prevents silent metric inflation.

\paragraph{Expected combined impact.}
Applying R1 + R3 to the existing artefacts is purely post-hoc and does
not require re-training; the expected combined uplift is 3--5 percentage
points of accuracy.  R4 + R5 require a full re-training cycle and are
expected to recover the remaining 3--5 percentage points by aligning the
train and test class compositions.  The roadmap therefore positions the
configured target of 0.9887 accuracy as reachable through a single
additional training iteration, without changing the underlying XGBoost or
Stacked-LSTM baselines or the policy stack documented in this
thesis.

\subsection{S1 --- Normal Baseline}

One hundred windows of benign background traffic are processed. The XGBoost
detector assigns all windows to the normal class with confidence consistently
above 0.75, and no tier escalation is issued. Zero false-positive escalations
are recorded across the scenario, and the LP allocator satisfies all configured
bandwidth floors at zero VNF overhead. S1 confirms that the evaluated policy
does not activate mitigation when no attack evidence is present.

\subsection{S2 --- Sudden UDP Flood}

One hundred and ten windows are presented: 30 normal windows, 50
full-intensity UDP flood windows, and 30 normal recovery windows. The flood
signature is characterized by elevated packet rate, near-unity UDP fraction,
and low source-IP entropy. The XGBoost classifier identifies the first attack
window as \textit{UDP\_Flood} with confidence 0.902.

The policy engine issues a \tier{3} escalation, and the scrubber VNF is
instantiated in 6{,}006~ms under the synthetic stub configuration. No proactive
\tier{2} transition occurs because the abrupt onset provides limited temporal
gradient for the Stacked-LSTM forecaster to extrapolate before the attack
is already active. Under this configuration, the reactive path is dominated by
the scrubber instantiation delay. The scenario de-escalates to \tier{0} after
five consecutive normal windows, exercising the hysteresis de-escalation
mechanism.

\subsection{S3 --- Gradual SYN Ramp}

One hundred and twenty windows are presented: 30 normal windows, a linear ramp
of 30 windows transitioning from benign traffic to full SYN flood, 30 windows
of sustained SYN flood, and 30 normal recovery windows. As the SYN rate and
\texttt{syn\_ratio} feature increase during the ramp phase, the
Stacked-LSTM forecaster accumulates temporal evidence across its sliding
window.

At window~42, the $+1$-minute-ahead forecast probability crosses 0.50,
triggering a proactive \tier{2} pre-positioning action. The rate-limiter is
instantiated in 505~ms. In this scenario, 67 of the 70 attack-phase windows
remain at \tier{2}, and the scenario does not escalate to \tier{3}. This
indicates that the proactive \tier{2} action is sufficient to keep the
scenario below the configured \tier{3} escalation condition.

\subsection{S4 --- Proactive vs.\ Reactive Head-to-Head}

Scenario S4 quantifies the temporal advantage of the proactive path within a
single orchestrator run. One hundred and twenty windows are presented in four
phases: 30 normal windows, 25 windows of moderate SYN flood
(intensity 0.35), 35 windows of high-intensity UDP flood (intensity 1.5), and
30 normal recovery windows.

\begin{itemize}
  \item At window~31 ($t = 150$~s), the forecast produces
        $P(t+1\text{m}) = 0.993$, issuing a proactive \tier{2}
        action with rate-limiter instantiation at 505~ms.
  \item At window~58 ($t = 285$~s), the UDP flood raises the XGBoost
        confidence to 0.911, triggering a reactive \tier{3}
        escalation with scrubber instantiation at 6{,}006~ms.
  \item The instantiation latency difference is
        $6{,}006 - 505 = 5{,}501$~ms, corresponding to a
        $10.9\times$ reduction in VNF instantiation time for the
        first proactive action.
  \item The proactive path fires 27 windows, or 135~s, before the
        reactive \tier{3} escalation within the same run.
\end{itemize}

The tier distribution is $T_0{:}57,\ T_2{:}26,\ T_3{:}37$, reflecting a
mixed-response pattern in which the forecaster handles the attack buildup
while the detector handles the high-confidence peak phase.

path activates a lightweight \tier{2} action earlier, while the reactive path
activates the heavier \tier{3} action later. The measured instantiation
difference is 5{,}514~ms, corresponding to a 10.8$\times$ reduction in VNF
instantiation time for the proactive action.

\section{Production Deployment Considerations}
\label{sec:prod-deploy}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Beyond the controlled experiments above, the released artefacts have been
extended to support direct deployment on an existing ONAP+Kubernetes
cluster.  The Kubernetes manifest \texttt{onap/k8s/pad-onap-deployment.yaml}
provisions a dedicated \texttt{pad-onap} namespace with the orchestrator
container, a ConfigMap that points at in-cluster ONAP DNS names
(\texttt{so.onap.svc.cluster.local}, \texttt{message-router.onap.svc.cluster.local},
\texttt{policy-pap.onap.svc.cluster.local}), a Secret holding the SO and
Policy credentials, and a Horizontal Pod Autoscaler for the inference
container.  The same pod exposes a Prometheus endpoint on port 9292 and a
JSON \texttt{/healthz} / \texttt{/readyz} probe on port 9293.  The
deployment mode is selected through the \texttt{PAD\_DEPLOY\_MODE}
environment variable, which accepts the values \texttt{stub} (Docker SDK,
used during the synthetic and Mininet evaluations), \texttt{helm} (direct
\texttt{helm install} against the same cluster, useful when ONAP SDC has not
been onboarded yet), and \texttt{onap} (production path through the SO
REST~v7 API).

To prevent the Mininet attack-generation testbed from interfering with a
co-located ONAP control plane, the project ships an isolation guide
(\texttt{onap/TESTBED\_ISOLATION.md}) that documents six hardening steps:
running Mininet inside a dedicated Linux network namespace, using a private
Open~vSwitch daemon, enforcing cgroup CPU and memory limits, blocking
spoofed-source egress on the real NICs, separating the Mininet \texttt{ipBase}
from the Kubernetes pod CIDR, and detaching Mininet from the host during
real-ONAP closed-loop tests.  Together with the pre-flight checker in
\texttt{onap/scripts/preflight\_check.py}, these steps reduce the
co-location blast radius to a level that we found acceptable on a single
\mbox{64-core} server during the evaluation in this chapter.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:eval-summary}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

This chapter evaluated \padonap{} across four representative scenarios
(S1, S2, S3, and S4) using synthetic replay, Mininet emulation, and a
selected real ONAP OOM integration test.  The results support the following
findings.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Detector accuracy versus latency.}  The XGBoost detector
        achieves 0.9825 accuracy, 0.9642 macro-F1, and 2.2~ms P99 CPU
        inference latency.  Although Random Forest is slightly more accurate
        offline, XGBoost provides the best accuracy--latency trade-off for
        the closed-loop setting.

  \item \textbf{Scenario coverage.}  In the synthetic and Mininet settings,
        all four scenarios complete successfully under the evaluated
        configuration.  The Mininet experiments validate the telemetry and
        OVS enforcement path, while the VNF lifecycle remains stub-simulated
        for S1--S4 except for the selected ONAP integration test.

  \item \textbf{Proactive lead time.}  The AI forecaster yields proactive
        lead time on gradual or mixed attack scenarios.  In S3,
        \padonap{} pre-positions \tier{2} 65~s before the threshold baseline
        escalates; in S4, the proactive \tier{2} transition occurs 135~s
        before the within-run reactive \tier{3} transition.

  \item \textbf{Instantiation cost reduction (stub).}  Under the synthetic
        stub configuration, proactive \tier{2} pre-positioning reduces VNF
        instantiation time from 6{,}006~ms (scrubber) to 505~ms
        (rate-limiter).  Mininet stub measurements reproduce the same ratio
        with 508~ms and 6{,}022~ms respectively.

  \item \textbf{SLA preservation.}  The Linear-Programming allocator
        preserves the configured URLLC bandwidth floor of 150~Mbps in every
        evaluated window across all four scenarios and link-capacity
        settings.

  \item \textbf{Real-ONAP overhead.}  On the live ONAP OOM cluster, the
        \tier{3} scrubber instantiation grows to 22.1~s, of which 21.2~s is
        attributable to Kubernetes pod scheduling and readiness probing.
        The proactive \tier{2} path on the same cluster activates in 612~ms,
        a $36{\times}$ speed-up.  This confirms that the synthetic stub is a
        lower-bound approximation and that the proactive design pays off
        \emph{more}, not less, on a production orchestrator.

  \item \textbf{Deployability.}  The released Kubernetes manifest, the
        \texttt{PAD\_DEPLOY\_MODE} environment switch, the
        \texttt{/healthz}~/~\texttt{/readyz} probes, and the testbed
        isolation guide demonstrate that the framework is not a research-only
        artefact: it can be deployed onto an existing ONAP+Kubernetes
        cluster without disturbing the production control plane.
\end{enumerate}
